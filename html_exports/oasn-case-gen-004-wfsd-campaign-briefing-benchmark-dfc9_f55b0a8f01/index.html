World Food Safety DayPurpose

Briefing title

World Food Safety Day draws attention and inspires action.

Jointly facilitated by FAO and WHO

World Food Safety Day is jointly facilitated by the Food and Agriculture Organization of the United Nations (FAO) and the World Health Organization (WHO). The day aims to help prevent, detect, and manage foodborne risks.

The toolkit links this work to food security, human health, economic prosperity, agriculture, market access, tourism, and sustainable development.

Source PDF page 2

The toolkit frames food safety as a collective responsibility across the food chain, from producer to consumer.

PreventPrioritize safe food practices along the chain.
DetectUse surveillance, reporting, and clear channels.
ManageRespond quickly when the unexpected happens.

The toolkit states that these illnesses and deaths are largely preventable if food safety is prioritized along the food chain.

Target audiencesShared responsibility

Briefing title

Governments, food businesses, and consumers each manage a different part of readiness.

Governments

Set the response architecture: emergency plans, national food control systems, surveillance, coordination, and public communication.

Develop or update national food safety emergency response plans.
Strengthen control, surveillance, and coordination capacities.
Communicate with food businesses and the public.

Food businesses

Make preparedness operational through management plans, collaboration, lessons learned, and consumer communication.

Improve food safety management plans.
Share lessons learned and work collaboratively.
Improve communication with consumers.

Consumers

Understand how to report or respond to food safety incidents and how the unexpected can affect safety at home.

Know how to report or respond to an incident.
Understand implications of unexpected events at home.
Know how to react when food safety is compromised.

INFOSANRapid exchange

Briefing title

INFOSAN helps authorities exchange information quickly during food safety events.

The FAO/WHO International Food Safety Authorities Network facilitates rapid exchange of information during food safety events, enabling risk management measures to prevent foodborne illness and save lives.

2004

Codex revised principles and guidelines for exchange of information in food safety emergency situations.

189

countries represented in the global network, with members from national food safety authorities.

1000s

of food safety events supported through rapid information exchange since creation.

Campaign messagesPartner copy

Briefing title

Use messages that keep the tone prepared, factual, and collective.

Preparedness is key

Plans and surveillance systems are essential when the unexpected happens.

Rapid exchange of information saves lives

Fast, accurate sharing helps identify the nature and origin of food safety events.

Food safety management plans help protect consumers

Food operators should have established and regularly updated plans.

Multisectoral collaboration ensures effective response

A coordinated approach helps minimize public health impact.

Effective communication maintains confidence

Accurate, open, and timely information helps prevent further illness.

Decisive action of food operators benefits all

Emergency response plans, withdrawal, recall, and traceability help track food through the chain.

Everyone is a risk manager

Food safety is everyone's business. Individuals, families, communities, businesses, and governments all make risk decisions.

Activate the campaign by making preparedness practical and visible.
